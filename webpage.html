What is Gene Editing?

Gene Editing in a Nutshell

Gene editing is a powerful tool used to make highly specific changes to the DNA of a living cell. It allows scientists to "cut" the double helix at a precise location to either disable a harmful gene or insert a functional one.

The technology relies on specialized enzymes that act as molecular scissors. Once the DNA is cut, the cell's natural repair mechanisms kick in to stitch the gap back together, incorporating the desired changes in the process.

The History of Gene Correction

  • 1970s: Discovery of restriction enzymes, the first natural tools used to cut DNA at specific (though limited) sequences.
  • 1990s – 2000s: The era of "Programmable Scissors" began with Zinc Finger Nucleases (ZFNs) and TALENs, which allowed for more customization but were difficult to build.
  • 2012: The CRISPR-Cas9 revolution. Scientists repurposed a bacterial immune system into a simple, cheap, and highly accurate gene-editing platform.
  • Present: Research is shifting toward "Prime Editing" and "Base Editing," which allow for even finer control without breaking both strands of the DNA.

The Newest Edition: CRISPR

CRISPR is a gene-editing tool based on a natural system found in bacteria. It uses proteins like Cas9 to cut DNA at specific locations, allowing scientists to change genes with high precision. This makes it faster and more efficient than older genetic engineering methods.

It has major uses in medicine and agriculture. Scientists are testing CRISPR to treat diseases like sickle cell disease by fixing faulty genes, and to improve crops by making them more resistant to pests and harsh conditions.
